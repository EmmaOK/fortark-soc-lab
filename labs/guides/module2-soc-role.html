The SOC Analyst Role

Foundational

The alert on your screen at 2:17am

02:17 UTC — Your shift It is the middle of the night. You are the analyst on the overnight shift. Your screen shows a new alert:

Rule 2502 — Brute Force: syslog: User missed the password more than one time
Level 10 · Source IP: 140.82.114.4 · Target: lab-target · 13 failures in 60 seconds

You have 30 minutes to acknowledge this. Your organisation's contract says so.

You have three questions to answer before you can go back to your coffee:

1. Is this real, or a false alarm?
2. If it is real, how bad is it?
3. What do I do right now?

This is triage. It is the core skill of a Tier 1 SOC analyst. Everything in this course is designed to make you fast and accurate at it.

What is a SOC?

A Security Operations Centre (SOC) is a team — or a managed service — whose job is to monitor an organisation's systems for security threats 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, 365 days a year.

Large organisations build their own internal SOC. Smaller organisations outsource to a Managed Security Service Provider (MSSP) — a company that runs a SOC on their behalf for a monthly fee.

Either way, the SOC is the organisation's first line of defence against active threats. When something bad is happening right now, the SOC is the team that sees it first.

The tier structure

SOC teams are organised into tiers based on skill and responsibility:

Tier 1 — Alert Analyst

This is where most SOC careers start. Tier 1 analysts monitor the alert queue, triage incoming alerts, filter out false positives, and escalate confirmed or suspected incidents to Tier 2. Volume work — a busy Tier 1 analyst might triage 50–100 alerts per shift.

Tier 2 — Incident Responder

Tier 2 analysts investigate escalated incidents in depth. They dig into the full timeline, correlate data across multiple tools, determine the scope of a breach, and recommend or carry out containment actions. Deeper technical work, fewer cases.

Tier 3 — Threat Hunter / Senior Analyst

Tier 3 does not wait for alerts. They proactively search for threats that have not triggered any detection — attackers who are already inside but have not been caught yet. They also build and tune the detection rules that Tier 1 and Tier 2 rely on.

In the labs in this course, you are doing Tier 1 and Tier 2 work:
• Lab 1: Tier 1 triage — is this real? how bad? what next?
• Lab 2: Tier 2 investigation — what exactly did the attacker do after they got in?
• Lab 3 onwards: understanding how SOC tooling supports both tiers

What a Tier 1 shift actually looks like

A Tier 1 analyst on shift has an alert queue — a live list of alerts that have fired and need to be reviewed. The queue is prioritised by severity. Level 10+ alerts sit at the top.

For each alert, the analyst:

  1. Opens the alert — reads what rule fired, when, on which system, from which source
  2. Investigates context — is this source IP seen before? is this rule noisy on this machine? is this during business hours or 3am?
  3. Makes a decision — true positive (real threat), false positive (benign activity matching the rule), or needs more investigation
  4. Documents the decision — writes a triage note explaining what they found and why they made the call they made
  5. Takes action — closes the alert, escalates to Tier 2, blocks an IP, notifies the client

That last step — the documented decision — is not bureaucracy. It is the product. The next analyst on shift needs to understand what happened. A legal team may need it if the incident becomes a court case. A compliance auditor may review it. An incident post-mortem will use it.

Spotting the alert is the easy part. Writing a clear, accurate, defensible record of what you found and what you decided — that is what the job actually requires.

True positives and false positives

Every alert is one of four things:

Threat is real Threat is not real
Alert fired True Positive ✓ False Positive
Alert did not fire False Negative True Negative ✓

True Positive — The alert fired and there is a real threat. This is what you act on.

False Positive — The alert fired but it is benign activity. The rule matched something innocent. These are noise — and managing them is a significant part of the job.

False Negative — A real threat happened but no alert fired. The worst outcome — you never know.

True Negative — Nothing happened, no alert fired. Normal and expected.

Every detection rule is a balance between catching real threats (minimising false negatives) and not generating so many false positives that analysts stop paying attention. A rule that fires on everything catches everything — and analysts learn to ignore it within a week. A rule that almost never fires gets treated as urgent — but may miss things.

In Lab 1, you will verify a True Positive — the alert is real, the attack is real, and the triage note should record it as such.

What an SLA is and who it serves

An SLA (Service Level Agreement) is a contractual commitment about performance. In a SOC context, SLAs define how fast the team must respond to different types of alerts.

The organisation — the one paying for the SOC service — is not buying "someone will look at this eventually." They are buying a guaranteed response window. If the SOC misses that window, there are financial and reputational consequences.

A typical SOC SLA structure:

Severity Acknowledge Investigate Escalate
Low (1–6) Next business day 72 hours Not required
Medium (7–9) 4 hours 8 hours Tier 2 within 8 hours
High (10–11) 30 minutes 2 hours Tier 2 immediately
Critical (12–14) 15 minutes Immediate Management within 30 min
Emergency (15) Immediate Immediate CISO now

"Acknowledge" means an analyst has seen the alert and confirmed they are working it. The clock starts when the alert fires — not when you open your laptop.

This is why a level 10 alert at 2:17am matters. The SLA clock started at 2:17am. If no analyst acknowledges it by 2:47am, the SOC has breached its contract. This is why overnight shifts exist. This is why there is always someone watching.

The three triage questions — in order

When an alert arrives, work through these three questions in order. Do not jump to action before answering all three.

Question 1: Is this real?

Is this a true positive or a false positive? Look at the source IP — is it known and trusted? Look at the time — is this activity normal for this system at this hour? Look at the target — does the targeted username exist? Could legitimate activity generate this alert?

Question 2: How bad is it?

If it is real — what is the impact? Did the attack succeed? Is this a failed attempt or a confirmed breach? Is this an isolated event or part of a broader campaign? Which systems are affected?

Question 3: What next?

Given what you know — monitor, escalate, block, or contain? Who needs to know? What evidence needs to be preserved? What does the triage note say?

These questions are not sequential steps you do once. They are a loop. As you find more evidence, your answers change. A level 10 alert that looks like a false positive at first glance might become a confirmed breach once you check whether a successful login followed the failures.

The deliverable

At the end of every alert investigation, there is one required output: the triage note.

A triage note is not a long report. It is a structured, factual record of:

  • What happened (what the alert said)
  • What you found when you investigated (the evidence)
  • What decision you made (true positive / false positive / needs investigation)
  • What action you took or recommended
Date/Time   : 2026-05-30 13:38 UTC
Source IP   : 140.82.114.4
Target      : admin / victim @ lab-target
Alert hits  : rule 5710 x47  rule 2502 x3  rule 40112: YES
Outcome     : CONFIRMED BREACH — successful login after brute force
Verdict     : True Positive
Action      : Escalate to Tier 2 / Incident Response
Analyst     : [name]

Short. Factual. Complete. Any analyst who reads this in three months — or three years — should understand exactly what happened and exactly what decision was made.

This is the document you will write at the end of Lab 1.

Key concepts from this module

  • A SOC monitors for active threats 24/7 — the overnight shift exists because attackers do not take breaks
  • Tier 1 triages alerts, Tier 2 investigates incidents, Tier 3 hunts for undetected threats
  • Triage answers three questions in order: real or not? how bad? what next?
  • Every alert is a true positive, false positive, false negative, or true negative — knowing which one changes everything
  • SLAs are contractual — the clock starts when the alert fires, not when you see it
  • The triage note is the deliverable — the documented, defensible record of what happened and what you decided
