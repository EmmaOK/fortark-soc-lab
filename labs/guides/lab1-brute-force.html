What is a brute force attack?

Two terms you need to understand first
SSH — Secure Shell
A protocol that lets you log into a computer over a network — remotely, from anywhere in the world — by typing a username and password. SSH is legitimate technology used by system administrators every day. Attackers use it because once you log in, you have the same access as the person whose credentials you used.
Brute Force
Trying every possible password, one after another, until one works. By hand, a human can try 5 passwords per minute. With an automated tool like Hydra, an attacker can try 100 passwords per second. The attack does not require cleverness — just patience and a wordlist of common passwords. Most organisations have at least one weak account. That is the one the attacker finds.

Analogy: Imagine a burglar with a keyring of 10,000 keys standing at your front door, trying each one as fast as possible. Most will not fit. But if your lock uses one of the common cuts they have, they will get through — and you will never know they tried the other 9,999 until you check the camera footage. In this lab, you are running the automated keyring.

The scenario

02:17 UTC — SOC Alert It is 2:17am. Your SOC dashboard sends an alert to your phone.

A company server is being hammered with failed SSH login attempts — over a hundred in the last two minutes, all from the same IP address, all targeting the same username. The pattern is unmistakable: someone is running an automated password attack.

Your job as the analyst on shift is to answer three questions as fast as possible:

1. Is this real, or a false alarm?
2. Did they get in?
3. What do you do in the next 10 minutes?

In this lab, you are both the attacker and the analyst. You will launch the attack yourself from a Kali Linux terminal in your browser, watch the alerts appear in the SOC dashboard in real time, and triage what you found — exactly as a SOC analyst would on a real shift.

Part 2 — Launch the brute force

You are now the attacker.

The tool you will use is called Hydra — one of the most widely used password-cracking tools in the world, used by both attackers and security professionals. It takes a list of passwords and fires them at your target's SSH server one by one, automatically, until something works.

In the Kali terminal, run this command. Replace [N] with your student number:

hydra -l admin -P /usr/share/wordlists/rockyou.txt -t 4 -V -f ssh://s[N]-lab-target

What each flag does:

FlagWhat it does
-l adminTry to log in as the username admin
-P rockyou.txtUse this list of 14 million real-world leaked passwords
-t 4Run 4 attempts at the same time
-VPrint every attempt to the screen as it happens
-fStop the moment a working password is found
ssh://s[N]-lab-targetConnect to your target machine directly by hostname

You will see output like this scrolling fast:

[ATTEMPT] target lab-target - login "admin" - pass "123456"
[ATTEMPT] target lab-target - login "admin" - pass "password"
[ATTEMPT] target lab-target - login "admin" - pass "iloveyou"

Note the exact time, then switch straight to your Wazuh tab.

Part 3 — Hunt the alerts

Wave 1 — Individual failures (rule 5710)

Within 5–10 seconds of the attack starting, alerts will begin appearing. Each one represents a single failed SSH login attempt that Wazuh caught and turned into a security event.

Click any alert to expand it and find:

Q1
What IP address is the attack coming from? (field: data.srcip)
Q2
What username is being targeted? (field: data.srcuser)
Q3
The alert message says "Invalid user admin" — what does that mean, and why does it matter?
There is a critical difference between two alerts that both look like "failed login":

"Invalid user admin" — the username admin does not exist on this machine. The attacker does not even have a valid username yet.

"Failed password for root" — the username root exists. The attacker has the right username. They just need the password.

Same alert type. Completely different risk level. A good analyst reads the message, not just the rule ID.

Wave 2 — Brute force pattern detected (rule 2502)

After roughly 8 failures from the same IP, Wazuh recognises the pattern and fires a higher-severity alert. It has correlated the individual failures into a single attack story.

Update your filter:

agent.name: s[N]-lab-target AND rule.id: 2502
Q4
What level does rule 2502 fire at? (check rule.level)
Q5
How many failures triggered it?
Wazuh rule levels run from 0 to 15. Level 5 is low, level 10 is high, level 12 and above is critical. In most SOC environments anything above level 7 triggers a notification to the on-call analyst. A level 10 brute force alert at 2am would wake someone up.

SOC SLAs by alert level — real organisations are contractually bound to these timelines:

Level Severity Acknowledge Investigate Escalate if unresolved
1–6 Low / Info Next business day Within 72 hours Not required
7–9 Medium Within 4 hours Within 8 hours Tier 2 within 8 hours
10–11 High Within 30 minutes Within 2 hours Tier 2 immediately
12–14 Critical Within 15 minutes Immediate Tier 2 + management within 30 min
15 Emergency Immediate Immediate CISO + incident commander now

Rule 2502 fires at level 10 — that is a 30-minute acknowledgement SLA. The clock starts the moment the alert fires, not when an analyst notices it. In this lab that alert appeared within 10 seconds of hydra starting. In a real SOC, you would already be 10 seconds into your 30-minute window.

Part 4 — Simulate the breach

The username admin does not exist on your target. Hydra will try every password in the list and fail — you cannot log into an account that does not exist.

But real attackers adapt. When one username fails, they try others gathered from reconnaissance. They find that the username victim exists on the system.

Stop hydra with Ctrl+C in the Kali terminal.

Now simulate the attacker succeeding — log in as the real account:

ssh victim@s[N]-lab-target
# password: password123

Once inside, type exit. You only needed the session to be established.

Back in Wazuh, search for:

agent.name: s[N]-lab-target AND rule.id: 40112
Q6
Is there a rule 40112 alert? What level is it?
Rule 40112 is the most important alert in this lab.

It fires when Wazuh sees a pattern of failures from an IP followed by a successful login. It does not just tell you someone tried to get in. It tells you: they got in.

This single alert changes everything. You are no longer investigating an attempted breach. You are investigating a confirmed breach. The response is completely different — isolation, forensics, escalation — versus simply blocking an IP and moving on.

Notice the level progression: rule 5710 fires at level 5, rule 2502 at level 10, rule 40112 at level 12. Wazuh is automatically escalating severity as the attack story becomes clearer.

Key takeaways

  • Brute force is loud — hundreds of attempts in minutes is an obvious pattern. The only question is whether anyone is watching when it happens.
  • Always check whether the attack succeeded — a pile of failed logins is noisy and common. Failed logins followed by a success is a confirmed breach requiring a completely different response.
  • "Invalid user" vs "failed password" are not the same thing — one means the attacker does not have a valid username yet. The other means they do. Read the alert message, not just the rule ID.
  • Rule 40112 is the one that matters — it does the correlation for you. When it fires, the attack story is complete. When it does not fire, keep watching.
  • The triage note is the deliverable — spotting the alert is the easy part. Writing a clear, accurate, documented decision that holds up under review is what the job actually requires.
  • Claude is a force multiplier, not an oracle — it produces an incident narrative in seconds. But your knowledge of the environment will always matter more than raw alerts. Use both.
